## 1. Invariant Subspaces (Null Space and Range)

Suppose $T \in \mathcal{L}(V)$ and $U$ is a subspace of $V$.

1a) Prove that $U \subseteq \text{null } T$, then $U$ is invariant under $T$.
1b) Prove that if $\text{range } T \subseteq U$, then $U$ is invariant under $T$.

**Solution:**
1a) Suppose $u \in U \subseteq \text{null } T$: Then $T(u) = 0$, but $0 \in U$.
1b) Suppose $u \in U$. If $U \supseteq \text{range } T$, then because $T(u) \in \text{range } T \subseteq U$, we have $T(u) \in U$.

## 2. Sum of Invariant Subspaces

Suppose that $T \in \mathcal{L}(V)$ and $V_1, \dots, V_m$ are subspaces of $V$ invariant under $T$. Prove that $V_1 + \dots + V_m$ is invariant under $T$.

**Solution:**
Suppose $u \in V_1 + \dots + V_m$: Then $u = v_1 + \dots + v_m$ with $v_k \in V_k$ and so
$$
T(u) = T(v_1 + \dots + v_m) = T(v_1) + \dots + T(v_m)$$
but $T(v_k) \in V_k$ because they are invariant, and therefore $T(u) \in V_1 + \dots + V_m$, and $V_1 + \dots + V_m$ is invariant.

## 3. Intersection of Invariant Subspaces

Suppose $T \in \mathcal{L}(V)$. Prove that the intersection of every collection of subspaces of $V$ invariant under $T$ is invariant under $T$.

**Solution:**
Suppose $u \in \bigcap_{i \in I} V_i$ with $V_i$ invariant under $T$, then $T u \in \bigcap_{i \in I} \text{range}(T|_{V_i})$. Because $V_i$ is invariant under $T$: $\text{range}(T|_{V_i}) \subseteq V_i$ for all $i \in I$ and so
$$
T u \in \bigcap_{i \in I} V_i$$

## 4. Subspaces Invariant Under All Operators

Prove or give a counterexample: If $V$ is finite-dimensional and $U$ is a subspace of $V$ that is invariant under every operator on $V$, then $U = \{0\}$ or $U = V$.

**Solution:**
Suppose there exist a subspace $U$ that is invariant under $T$ for all $T \in \mathcal{L}(V)$ but $U \neq \{0\}$ and $U \neq V$. Then there exists a $u \in U$ with $u \neq 0$ and a $v \in V \setminus U$. Then there exists an operator $T$ with $T(u) = v$ and $T(w) = 0$ for the other basis vectors $\{u, w_1, \dots, w_{m-1}\}$ of $V$, and $m = \dim V$. This operator is well defined, but $U$ is of course not invariant to this $T$, in contradiction to our hypothesis. Therefore no such subspace exists.

## 5. Eigenvalues of $T: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$

Suppose $T \in \mathcal{L}(\mathbb{R}^2)$ is defined by $T(x, y) = (-3y, x)$. Find the eigenvalues of $T$.

**Solution:**
$$(-3y, x) = \lambda (x, y) \implies -3y = \lambda x \text{ and } x = \lambda y$$
$$\implies -3y = \lambda (\lambda y) = \lambda^2 y \implies -3 = \lambda^2$$
This operator has no real eigenvalues.

## 6. Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors in $\mathbb{F}^2$

Define $T \in \mathcal{L}(\mathbb{F}^2)$ by $T(w, z) = (z, w)$. Find all eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $T$.

**Solution:**
$$(z, w) = \lambda (w, z): \quad z = \lambda w, \quad w = \lambda z$$
$$z = \lambda^2 z, \quad 1 = \lambda^2 \implies \lambda_1 = 1$$
For $\lambda = 1$: $(z, w) = 1 \cdot (w, z) \implies z = w \text{ and } w = z \implies v_1 = (x, x)$
For $\lambda = -1$: $(z, w) = -1 \cdot (w, z) \implies z = -w \text{ and } w = -z \implies v_2 = (-x, x)$

## 7. Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors in $\mathbb{F}^3$

Define $T \in \mathbb{F}^3$ by $T(z_1, z_2, z_3) = (2z_2, 0, 5z_3)$. Find all eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $T$.

**Solution:**
$$2z_2 = \lambda z_1, \quad 0 = \lambda z_2, \quad 5z_3 = \lambda z_3 \implies \lambda_1 = 5$$
For $\lambda = 5$: $(2z_2, 0, 5z_3) = 5(z_1, z_2, z_3) \implies v_1 = (0, 0, x)$ and

(If $\lambda=0$, then $2z_2=0 \implies z_2=0$. $z_1$ is free. $v_2 = (x, 0, 0)$).
(If $\lambda=2$, then $2z_2=2z_1$ and $0=2z_2$, so $z_1=z_2=0$. $v_3 = (0, 0, z_3)$ which corresponds to $\lambda=5$ again, suggesting $\lambda=2$ is not a true eigenvalue unless we allow $z_1 \ne 0$ which contradicts $0 = 2z_2$ when $\lambda=2$. Examining $0 = \lambda z_2$: if $\lambda \neq 0$, then $z_2=0$. If $z_2=0$, then $2z_2 = \lambda z_1$ gives $0 = \lambda z_1$. If $\lambda \neq 0$, then $z_1=0$. Thus, for $\lambda \neq 0, 5$, we must have $z_1=z_2=0$, giving $\lambda=5$. The eigenvalues are $5$ and $0$.)

If $\lambda=0$: $2z_2=0 \implies z_2=0$. $0=0\cdot z_2$. $5z_3=0\cdot z_3 \implies z_3=0$. Eigenvector $v_2 = (x, 0, 0)$.
